Why These Systems Hallucinate

It sounds confident even when it's wrong. Here's why that happens — and the one habit that makes the technology safe to use.

Step 1 of 7
The root cause

It produces what's likely — not what's true

🎯 most likely next word
vs
✔️ the correct word

A model has no built-in lie detector. It chooses the most plausible next token — and only hopes plausible and true are the same thing.

Good news first

Usually, likely and true line up

Because the model has read so much accurate writing, accurate writing is usually what it produces. Most answers are fine.

Where it breaks

Ask for a precise fact it never learned…

"Cite the exact clause and date of that regulation."
↓
"Section 14(b), enacted 12 March 2019." ⚠ confidently invented

With no real answer to draw on, it fills the gap with something that sounds right — delivered just as confidently as a true one.

Name it

This is a hallucination

💭
invented citation misattributed quote non-existent rule plausible-but-wrong date

Hallucinations are routine, not exotic — and not a bug awaiting a patch. They're a property of how the system works.

Definition · Hallucination

A confident assertion that isn't supported by reality. The goal isn't to eliminate them — it's to design your workflow so any hallucination is caught before it leaves your desk.

The professional rule

Delegate the doing — not the deciding-what's-true

✅ Safe to delegate
drafting · structuring · rephrasing · brainstorming · summarising
🙋 Stays with you
verifying every fact, date, citation and figure before it's used

Anywhere truth matters more than fluency, you cannot hand off the checking. That part is yours.

Build the habit

Put a verification gate in your workflow

🤖

Nothing leaves your desk until it passes the gate. The fact-check habit isn't a limit on the tool — it's what makes the tool usable at all.

Remember this

Confident ≠ correct

Treat AI as a brilliant, fast, occasionally over-confident assistant — never as a source of record.

Let it accelerate your thinking. Keep the final say — and the fact-checking — firmly in human hands.

🧭 A good test: "If this single fact were wrong, would it matter?" If yes — verify it yourself.
